The Forward Pass

Your prompt is now a stack of vectors — one per token, each a list of a few thousand numbers. What receives them is surprisingly repetitive in shape. A transformer is a tall pile of identical layers, typically some dozens of them, and every layer contains the same two sublayers. The first is attention, the only place where tokens exchange information with one another. The second is a small feed-forward network — an MLP — which transforms each token's vector on its own, paying no attention to its neighbors. That is essentially the entire architecture, applied over and over.

The way the layers connect matters as much as what they compute. Each token's vector flows upward through the stack along what is called the residual stream. A sublayer does not replace the vector; it reads it, computes a small correction, and adds that correction back in. The vector that leaves the top of the stack is the original embedding plus dozens of accumulated refinements — a running draft that gets annotated on every floor but never rewritten from scratch. Every token in your context makes this trip at the same time — the layers process all positions in parallel, which is a large part of why GPUs suit this work so well. And while the layers are identical in shape, each has its own learned weights, so each adds a different kind of refinement to the stream.

Attention

Attention deserves a closer look, because it is where the reading actually happens. The mechanism is simpler than its reputation. From its current vector, each token computes three new vectors by multiplying against three learned matrices: a query — roughly, "here is what I am looking for" — a key — "here is what I contain" — and a value — "here is what I will hand over if asked." A token's query is compared against the key of every token before it, one dot product per pair. The scores pass through a softmax, which turns them into positive weights summing to one, and the token then pulls in a mixture of the other tokens' values in exactly those proportions — heavily from tokens whose keys matched its query, faintly from everyone else. That weighted mixture is the correction added to its residual stream.

Two details complete the picture. First, causality: a token may only attend backward, to positions that come before it, never forward — the future is masked out. Second, each layer runs many of these operations side by side, in separate heads, each with its own query, key, and value matrices, and each free to learn its own notion of what is relevant to what. Nothing in the model knows what a pronoun is, and no rule about anaphora is written down anywhere; the behavior is just dot products between learned vectors that happened, during training, to make next-token prediction easier. Multiply this by dozens of heads and dozens of layers, and "reading your prompt" dissolves into these little vector comparisons repeated tens of thousands of times. One distribution

At the top of the stack, one more multiplication. The final vector of the last token is projected against the unembedding — a matrix with one row per entry in the vocabulary — producing one score per token: the logits. A last softmax converts them into probabilities. It is worth pausing on the asymmetry here. A machine with billions of parameters has just run end to end, layer by layer, across your whole context, and everything it bought is exactly one thing: a probability distribution over the next token. Not an answer, not a sentence. One distribution.

Something still has to turn that distribution into a choice. Always taking the single most likely token — greedy decoding — sounds sensible but produces stilted, repetitive text that easily falls into loops. Instead, the next token is sampled at random from the distribution, after reshaping it with a temperature: the logits are divided by T before the softmax. As T approaches zero the distribution sharpens toward the deterministic favorite; as it grows the distribution flattens toward noise. A companion trick, top-p, discards the long tail of barely-plausible tokens before sampling. The KV cache

"Runs again" should worry you. To produce token N + 1, attention needs the keys and values of all N earlier tokens — and computed naively, every step would recompute all of them, even though they depend only on the unchanged prefix and come out identical every time. So they are computed once and kept: every token's keys and values, for every layer and head, are stored in accelerator memory as the KV cache. Each new step computes a query, key, and value only for the newest token, attends over the cached keys and values of everything before it, and appends its own K and V to the cache.

This one optimization shapes the economics of everything you feel as a user. The cache is large — for a big model, a few hundred kilobytes per token, so a long agent session's context runs to gigabytes — and it must be streamed out of memory on every single step, which is why generating tokens is limited by memory bandwidth rather than arithmetic, and why long contexts respond more slowly and cost more. It is also precisely the thing that prompt caching, which we met in the datacenter section, preserves between the calls of the agent loop: the conversation's keys and values stay warm so the next request need not rebuild them. Production models refine this picture in ways we are skipping: grouped-query attention shares keys and values across heads to shrink the cache, mixture-of-experts layers route each token to a small subset of the MLP weights, and speculative decoding drafts several tokens cheaply and verifies them in a single pass. None of it changes the shape of the story told here.

Either way, at the end of the pass there now exists a token id that did not exist a moment ago — and it has to make its way back out of the datacenter to your terminal.
