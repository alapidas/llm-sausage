The Forward Pass

Your words are now long lists of numbers, about to enter the part that does the real thinking. Picture a tall assembly line: the numbers rise through a row of identical stations, and each does the same small job, looking at the work so far and nudging the guess a little closer.

In the demonstration below, follow one word up the stack as each station blends a small change into it.

Your prompt is now a stack of vectors, one per token. A transformer is a pile of dozens of identical layers, each with the same two sublayers: attention, the only place tokens exchange information, and a feed-forward MLP that transforms each token's vector on its own.

How they connect matters as much. Each vector flows up the residual stream: a sublayer never replaces it but reads it, adds a small correction, and passes it on, so the vector leaving the top is the embedding plus dozens of refinements. Below, three tokens ride their streams through an eight-layer stack; the slider follows one as each sublayer merges in its contribution.

Your prompt is now a matrix of shape [sequence, d_model], fed through a stack of identical transformer blocks. The organizing idea is the residual stream: a block never overwrites a token's vector but carries it through, while every sublayer reads a normalized copy, computes an update, and adds it back — writing x for the stream, x = x + attn(norm(x)) then x = x + mlp(norm(x)). That uninterrupted identity path lets gradients reach the bottom cleanly in training, so each layer need only learn a correction.

The norm is typically RMSNorm applied before each sublayer — pre-norm — keeping the stream's scale stable where post-norm drifts. The MLP holds most of the parameters: it projects d_model up to a wider inner dimension, applies a nonlinearity, and projects back, so its weights dwarf the attention weights. Because each vector rises independently, all positions run in parallel, a reason the work suits a GPU. Attention

Attention is simpler than its reputation. Each token forms three vectors against learned matrices — a query ("what I seek"), a key ("what I hold"), a value ("what I give") — and its query is dotted against the key of every token up to itself. A softmax turns those scores into weights summing to one, and the token pulls in a mixture of the others' values in those proportions, the correction added to its residual stream.

Two details complete it: a token may attend only to itself and earlier positions, never the future, and each layer runs many such operations in parallel heads. In the demonstration below, hover or tap any word to see where it attends under three hand-made heads.

Attention, the only sublayer that mixes information across positions, is built from three learned projections of the normalized stream: Q = xW_Q, K = xW_K, V = xW_V. The output is the scaled dot-product form softmax(QKᵀ / √d) V, with d the head dimension: each query dots against every key, the √d divisor keeps the scores from saturating the softmax, and the weights average the values. Causal masking sets future positions' pre-softmax scores to −∞, so position i attends only to positions ≤ i and can be trained without seeing its future.

This runs as multi-head attention: the projections split into independent heads, each in its own d-dimensional subspace, so heads specialize in different relations. Grouped-query attention, now common, lets many query heads share one key/value head — barely denting quality while shrinking the key/value footprint several-fold, key to the cache ahead. Order enters here too, not as a position vector at the input but as rotary embeddings (RoPE): each query and key is rotated by an angle proportional to position, so their dot product depends only on relative offset. After the words travel up the line, the machine produces one guess at the next word — a ranked list of possibilities with odds, not a finished answer. To pick, it rolls a weighted die rather than always grabbing the favorite, which makes stiff, repetitive writing. A setting called temperature controls how adventurous the roll is.

One distribution

At the top, one more multiplication. The final vector of the last token is projected against the unembedding, one row per vocabulary entry, giving one score per token — the logits — which a softmax turns into probabilities. Billions of parameters have run across your whole context to produce one distribution over the next token.

Something must turn it into a choice. Greedy decoding — always taking the top token — falls into repetitive loops, so the next token is sampled after dividing the logits by a temperature T: small T sharpens toward the favorite, large T flattens toward noise. Top-p first drops the long tail of barely-plausible tokens. Below, drag the temperature slider to reshape the bars, toggle top-p, then press Sample to draw the next token.

At the top the residual stream is normalized once more and multiplied by the unembedding matrix, of shape [d_model, vocab] — often the input embedding transposed and reused, saving a large parameter table. For generation only the last position matters: its final vector becomes the logits, one score per vocabulary entry — the entire output of the forward pass, a single unnormalized distribution over the next token.

Turning it into text is a separate, cheap step. Dividing the logits by a temperature T before the softmax rescales the distribution — T → 0 collapses it toward the argmax, larger T flattens it toward uniform — while top-p, or nucleus, sampling truncates to the smallest set of tokens whose probability sums to p. Greedy decoding, that T → 0 limit, tends to loop, so a modest temperature with top-p is the usual compromise. Choosing one word is only the start: the whole trip must happen again for the next, and again after that. Redoing it all each time would be wasteful, so the machine keeps a growing pile of notes about every word it has read and reuses them.

The KV cache

"Runs again" should worry you. To produce token N + 1, attention needs the keys and values of all N earlier tokens, which depend only on the unchanged prefix — so they are computed once and kept in memory as the KV cache. Each step computes a query, key, and value for the newest token alone, attends over the cached rest, and appends its own.

This shapes the economics you feel. The cache is large — a few hundred kilobytes per token, so a long session runs to gigabytes — and every step must stream it, and the far larger weights, out of memory. Moving those bytes, not the arithmetic, limits generation speed and is why long contexts are slower and costlier; prompt caching keeps that state warm between turns. Below, watch the cache grow one column per step, and flip the toggle to see what the naive world recomputes.

Producing each token re-runs the whole stack, but the prefix's keys and values never change, so recomputing them is waste. They are computed once and retained — the KV cache — and each step projects Q, K, V for the newest token alone, attends over the cached history, and appends its own. The cache size per token is about 2 × layers × kv_heads × head_dim × bytes, the leading 2 for keys and values alike.

Take 80 layers, 8 key/value heads after grouped-query attention, head dimension 128, in 2-byte bf16: 2 × 80 × 8 × 128 × 2 ≈ 320 KB per token, so a 100,000-token context holds roughly 32 GB of cache beside the weights. Without grouped-query attention — say 64 key/value heads — it would need eight times as much. The sizes are illustrative; the consequence is general: generation is bound by memory bandwidth, not arithmetic. Every step streams the weights and the whole cache out of high-bandwidth memory, and moving those bytes sets the pace — why long contexts cost more and run slower. Prompt caching keeps that state warm between turns. Either way, a brand-new word now exists that did not a moment ago, and it still has to find its way back out of the datacenter and onto your screen.

Production models add refinements we skip: grouped-query attention shares keys and values across heads to shrink the cache, mixture-of-experts layers route each token to a subset of the MLP weights, and speculative decoding drafts several tokens cheaply and verifies them in one pass. Token order enters by rotating the queries and keys by position, a scheme called RoPE. None of it changes the shape of the story.
